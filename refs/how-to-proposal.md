# How to Write a Thesis Proposal

Before starting your thesis, you must submit a proposal. This document outlines the problem you aim to address, the motivation for solving it, your key objectives, and a preliminary schedule. The proposal serves as a high-level overview, providing necessary details while remaining concise—aim for 5-7 pages total. Treat it like an elevator pitch: explain the issue to someone familiar with technical concepts but unfamiliar with your specific topic.

Incorporate visual aids such as figures and UML diagrams to clarify your points. Choose diagrams that best suit your topic, but keep them high-level and focused on describing the problem (not solutions). Include at least two diagrams or mockups.

Support your arguments with scientific sources. Cite at least 6-8 peer-reviewed publications to demonstrate your thorough understanding of the field.

Use the official Proposal and Thesis Template from GitHub: https://github.com/ls1intum/thesis-template-typst

## Proposal Structure
The expected length is 4-6 text pages (excluding metadata, figures, tables, and the schedule).

### Abstract
Provide a short summary (1/3 to 1/2 page) of the entire project. Repetition from other sections is acceptable here to ensure completeness.

### Introduction
Introduce the general context without delving into the problem. Describe the environment and tools involved. Keep this under one page.

### Problem
Clearly state the problem(s). Identify key actors (e.g., users, systems) and explain how the issue negatively impacts them. Avoid suggesting solutions or alternatives. Detail the consequences thoroughly, but limit to 3/4 page.

### Motivation
Explain the scientific importance of solving the problem. Reference actors to highlight benefits, but stay high-level. Focus on positive outcomes rather than restating the problem. Be visionary about potential impacts. Optionally, draw on existing research or prior work. Limit to 3/4 page.

### Objectives
Outline the main goals of your thesis. Begin with a brief introduction and a bulleted overview (3-4 high-level objectives, numbered 1, 2, 3, etc.; each fitting on one line and not overly general). Follow with subsections for each objective: restate the bullet point, then provide at least two paragraphs of detailed explanation.

### Schedule
Specify the thesis start date. Divide the timeline into iterations of 2-4 weeks each. For each iteration, list high-level, realistic work items that are measurable and deliverable—focusing on vertically integrated features. Exclude thesis writing or presentation tasks. Adhere to agile principles: avoid a dedicated requirements-gathering iteration. Instead, assume a backlog and select items per sprint, planning during the sprint. Reference objectives from the previous section to tie features to goals.

### Bibliography
Include only scientific, peer-reviewed sources (conference papers, journal articles, books). Non-scientific references go in footnotes. For theses (e.g., dissertations, master's, bachelor's), specify the type and university (see example: https://github.com/ls1intum/thesis-template-typst/issues/82).

### Transparency Statement
If you used AI tools, include a first-person statement detailing their use (e.g., "I used ChatGPT to generate initial drafts for the abstract and introduction."). Be specific—avoid generic templates. Always include: "I have carefully checked all texts created with these tools to ensure that they are correct and make sense."

## General Hints
- A strong proposal features at least two figures with detailed, descriptive captions. Reference them in-text (e.g., "Figure 1 shows..."). One must be a relevant UML diagram; the other can be a screenshot, chart, image, or another UML diagram.
- Limit text to 4-6 pages (excluding metadata, figures, tables, schedule).
- Maintain consistency: Use the same terms throughout.
- Break long text into paragraphs of 5-8 lines. Each should focus on one idea; avoid mixing topics. Keep lengths balanced for readability.
- For screenshots, use light mode only.
- Ensure figures are clear—prefer vector graphics over blurry images.
- Apply title case to all subsections, headlines, and titles.
